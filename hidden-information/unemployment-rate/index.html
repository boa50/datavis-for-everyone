Calculating the unemployment rate in many countries is somehow tricky, not because the equation is very complex but because the underlying data makes it very hard to reach a reasonable conclusion from it.

For instance, in Brazil, to get the unemployment rate, we can use the following formula:

Unemployment Rate = Not Working Population Not Working Population + Working Population

However, this formula takes into consideration some premises:

  • Only people who are part of the workforce are considered for this calculation
  • People not working are only the ones without a job that are currently looking for a job

So, people who don't want to work and those who are working only sporadically are not considered unemployed, making the final number a bit less worrisome.

You can play with the controls below to make the numbers more enjoyable for your presentation or to check how manipulative this information could be.

For example, if we would make some changes in the numbers for Brazil's case, we could take the following considerations to the out-of-workforce population:

  • Leave out of the workforce only people who receive benefits from our social security system (38M people; change value to 58%)
  • Let's say that at least one person of the families that receive money from our social programs is entirely unable to work (plus 21M people; change value to 89%)

This shows a slight change in the result and how anyone with power can change the unemployment rate, distorting the real purpose of the metric.

So, be careful whenever you see any metric exposed to you; it might contain some grey zones created to deceive you into thinking you know the truth.
